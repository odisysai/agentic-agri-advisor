# Cotton
## Overview
India is the world's largest cotton producer and second-largest consumer. The most common upland variety, *Gossypium hirsutum*, dominates Indian cultivation (especially Maharashtra's black cotton soil). Cotton is a commercial crop with high economic value but also requires careful pest management — it's considered one of the most pesticide-intensive crops globally, making safe and smart application critical.

## Water Management
Cotton needs moderate water but is sensitive to both drought and waterlogging.

| Stage | Water Requirement | Notes |
|-------|------------------|-------|
| Pre-sowing | 1-2 light irrigations to prepare soil moisture | Depends on monsoon onset timing; in Krishna/Wardha valley delay sowing to June 15 |
| Seedling/Squaring (0-50 days) | Light irrigation every 15-20 days; avoid deep standing water | High soil moisture = excessive vegetative growth, boll drop |
| Flowering/Boll set (50-110 days) | 6-8 irrigations of 40mm each; maintain field moisture | Critical phase — drought at first flowering can reduce yield by 40% |
| Boll opening (110+ days) | Minimal water; stop irrigation 3-4 weeks before harvest | Excess moisture during boll opening causes fiber quality decline and good fungal growth |

**Drip irrigation note:** Cotton is well-suited to drip with ** Fertigation ** (fertigation = injecting nutrients through irrigation). Water use efficiency increases 40-60% with drip vs flood. Monitor soil moisture at 15cm depth.

## Common Diseases
| Disease | Symptoms | Treatment Approach | Severity | Season |
|---------|----------|-------------------|----------|--------|
| Grey Mold (Botrytis cinerea) | White fuzzy cotton-like growth on boll and bracts; damp rot in bolls, fiber becomes grey | Copper-based fungicides (copper oxychloride); improve field ventilation; avoid excessive nitrogen | Moderate — 10-25% yield loss during boll stage | Kharif, summer (monsoon + post-monsoon) |
| Boll Rot Complex (Aspergillus, Fusarium) | Brown/black patching on boll interior; yellow fibers from seed coat contamination | Apply Mancozeb fungicides pre-emptively during boll opening; harvest promptly once bolls open | High — destroys fiber quality and market value | Summer (July-September) |
| Wilt (Fusarium oxysporum / Verticillium dahliae) | Plant wilts suddenly; leaves turn yellow then brown, lower branches affected first; root rot visible at taproot | Remove and burn infected plants; improve drainage; rotate with non-host crops (cereals, pulses); avoid prolonged standing water | High — no effective cure once roots infected; focus on prevention | Throughout season (post heavy rain) |
| YMV — Yam Unifolia Mosaic Virus | Mosaic mottling on leaves, downward cupping of leaf margins; stunting; no treatment available | Remove infected plants immediately; control whiteflies (aphid vectors) preventively with neem oil spray; use resistant varieties | Moderate — stunting reduces yield 30-50% if widespread | Summer (June-August) |

## Common Pests
| Pest | Symptoms/Damage | Treatment Approach | Severity |
|------|-----------------|-------------------|----------|
| Cotton Bollworm (Helicoverpa armigera) | Bores into bolls, destroys seeds; damaged boll opens prematurely with brown discoloration at entry hole | Apply chlorantraniliprole liquid or spinosad 2.5SC spray; use pheromone traps at 10/acre for early detection | High — India's #1 cotton pest; responsible for most crop loss |
| Pink Bollworm (Pectinophora gossypiella) | Enters boll through pinhole; pinkish threads visible; larvae inside destroy seeds, partial fiber contamination | Apply Bt-based products (Bacillus thuringiensis); use pheromone traps; remove and burn infested bolls | High — causes fiber quality contamination that reduces mill prices by 10-30% |
| Whitefly (Bemisia tabaci) — virus vector | Sucking sap, yellowing leaves, sticky honeydew on leaf underside; transmits YMV and CLCuV | Spray imidacloprid or chlorpyrifos 20EC drench; install yellow sticky traps at 10/acre | High — virus transmission is as damaging as the pest itself |
| Jassid (Amrasca biguttula) | Characteristic "hopperburn" — yellow-orange speckling of leaves from leaf-edge inward; if left unchecked causes complete leaf drying | Spray Deltamethrin 2.8 EC or use neem oil; plant resistant varieties (like Acala); protect ladybug and syrphid predators | Moderate to High — yields can drop 20-35% if not treated in early season |

## Bt Cotton Note
India has seen a shift to Bt cotton (genetically modified for bollworm resistance) since 2002. While Bt cotton has reduced bollworm damage significantly, it does NOT protect against:
- Pink bollworm (which can overcome early Bt toxins)
- Whitefly / Jassid (sap-sucking, not boll-boring insects)
- Yellowing/yellow vine of Bollworm

**Recommendation:** Always maintain non-Bt refugia (10-20% area of conventional cotton) to delay pest resistance evolution. Rotate Bt fields with non-Bt plots every 2-3 seasons.

## Safety Notes (Always Follow)
- ⚠️ Cotton is one of the most pesticide-intensive crops globally. Always follow product label instructions for dosage, mixing rate, and application method.
- ⚠️ Bt cotton does NOT protect against all pests — always monitor and apply integrated pest management (IPM).
- ⚠️ Wear protective gear (gloves, mask) during all pesticide applications.
- ⚠️ Observe the pre-harvest interval (PHI) listed on the product label before harvesting treated crops.
- ⚠️ Dispose of empty pesticide containers properly — never burn, do not dump near water sources.
- ⚠️ Avoid pesticide application during harvest/boll picking to prevent chemical contamination of fibers.
